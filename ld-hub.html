Role guide
Care coach
As a care coach, you play a key part in helping our care workers be the best they can be. This is not about telling people what to do — it is about understanding their challenges, creating space for reflection, and supporting them to improve.
✕
Observation visitsSkill gap identificationCoaching conversationsStructured feedbackDevelopment planningEscalation of concerns
1
Observe without intervening
Unless there is immediate risk, let the care worker work. Your job at this stage is to watch, not guide.
2
Ask before you tell
Begin every feedback conversation by asking what the care worker thought went well and what they would do differently.
3
Be specific, not general
"When you supported Mrs Jones I noticed you moved quickly without checking she was ready" is more useful than "you seemed rushed."
4
Agree next steps together
Actions the care worker has agreed to are more likely to be followed through than actions handed to them.
5
Follow up
Check in at the next visit. Did they try what you agreed? This closes the loop and builds trust over time.

Escalate immediately if there is any risk to the safety or dignity of a service user, a safeguarding concern of any kind, or a pattern of poor practice that has not improved despite coaching. Some situations need a manager — recognising that is part of doing the role well.

Frame concerns around the impact on the service user, not a judgement of the person. "I want to talk about what I observed because it could affect Mrs Jones's safety" lands very differently to "that wasn't good enough."

1
Choose the right moment
Not in front of the service user or other staff.
2
Describe what you saw, not what you think it means
Stick to what was observable.
3
Give them the opportunity to respond
There may be context you are not aware of.

Observation records must be factual, specific, and evidence-based. Write what you observed — not your general impression. The record should clearly show the outcome, what was done well, what needs improvement, and what happens next.

Situation

You are observing a new care worker during a moving and handling task. They are clearly hesitant — pausing frequently, not using the equipment with confidence, and visibly anxious. The service user picks up on it and becomes unsettled. Afterwards the care worker says they are not sure they are doing it right but did not want to say anything. What do you do?

The care worker has done the right thing by being honest afterwards — that matters and should be acknowledged. However, hesitancy during a moving and handling task is a safety concern that needs to be addressed before they carry out the task alone again.

Start by asking them to reflect on how they felt and what specifically they were unsure about. This gives you a clearer picture of whether it is a knowledge gap, a confidence issue, or both. Be honest that what you observed was not safe enough yet — but frame it as something you are going to work on together, not a failure.

Do not sign off the observation. Arrange a further practice session with the right equipment and support before they work unsupported. Record everything on Carefree and contact the office with your recommendations.

✓ Acknowledge the honesty, address the safety concern, arrange further support before they work unsupported.
Care assessor
As a care assessor, you play a key part in making sure service users receive the right care. The role goes beyond completing assessments — it is about working with service users, their families, and the wider team to truly understand what people need and making sure that is reflected in their care.
✕
New care plan assessmentsRisk assessmentsWelfare reviewsOn call duties
1
Prepare before you arrive
Review existing information, understand the referral reason, and know any risks or sensitivities.
2
Build rapport first
A comfortable person gives a far more accurate picture of their real needs.
3
Involve the person in the plan
The care plan must be agreed with the person, not written for them.
4
Implement any actions and book the next review before you leave
Do not leave it to be arranged later.
5
Complete all documentation on the day
Care plans, notes, and actions should be clear and accurate enough that anyone reviewing them has a full picture of the service user's needs.

Escalate immediately if a service user's needs have changed significantly, a safeguarding concern arises, or a situation requires urgent action. Do not sit on concerns — the sooner they are raised, the sooner they can be addressed.

Situation

You arrive for a six-week review. The service user's daughter is present and is doing most of the talking, answering questions on behalf of her mother. The service user appears to have capacity but seems reluctant to speak. What do you do?

The care plan must reflect what the service user wants — not what their family wants for them. Politely redirect the conversation back to the service user. Address questions directly to her, give her time to respond, and if needed ask the daughter if she would mind giving you a few minutes alone with her mother.

If the service user then communicates something different to what the daughter has been saying, that is significant and must be reflected in the care plan and escalated to your manager. Record everything accurately on Carefree.

✓ The service user's voice comes first — always.
Coordinator
As a coordinator, you play a key part in making sure the service runs smoothly — managing rotas, responding to last minute changes, and making sure service users receive their care safely and on time.
✕
Rota managementLast-minute gapsElectronic call monitoringStaff communicationOn call duties
1
Triage by risk
Rank outstanding issues by potential harm to a service user. Work through in order, not the order things arrive.
2
Communicate gaps early
If a call cannot be covered, escalate to the manager immediately.
3
Document every significant decision
A brief note at the time protects you and supports handover.
4
Stay calm on the outside
Care workers need to arrive at calls focused on the service user. Be steady, brief, and clear.

Escalate immediately if a high-risk call cannot be covered, a safeguarding concern is raised, or a service user cannot be contacted and welfare is uncertain. When in doubt, escalate — it is always better to raise something that turns out to be nothing than to sit on something that matters.

With care workers: be clear, direct, and respectful. Give them what they need without unnecessary detail.

With service users and families: be honest and calm — if there is a delay, tell them early. An explanation in advance is far better received than an apology after.

When escalating: give the situation, what you have already tried, and what you need. "Mrs Jones's 9am call is uncovered, I have tried all available staff, I need a decision" is more useful than "I can't sort this."

Situation

It is 7:45am. A care worker has not shown up for their 7am call with a service user who requires medication support. You cannot reach the care worker, have no available cover, and the manager is not answering their phone. What do you do?

Contact the service user or their family immediately to make them aware of the delay and check their welfare. Do not leave them without information.

Keep trying the manager through every available channel — mobile, office line, email. If there is an on-call number or a deputy, use it now. Log every attempt with the time. If you genuinely cannot reach anyone and the service user's safety is at risk, escalate further — and if necessary consider whether emergency services need to be involved. Document every decision and the time it was made.

✓ Contact the service user first, exhaust every escalation route, document everything.

Key principle
Fact find / investigation process
A fact find is a documented discussion — not a disciplinary process. The purpose is to understand what happened and decide what, if anything, needs to follow. Where a formal investigation is needed, the same principles of fairness and impartiality apply throughout.
✕

A fact find is a documented discussion. It is informal — no formal rights apply — but it must be respectful and the person must not feel ambushed. Its purpose is to understand, not to conclude.

1
Identify the concern clearly
Write down the specific concern before speaking to anyone. Vague concerns produce vague fact finds.
2
Gather initial information
Review records, rotas, care notes. Speak to witnesses if necessary. Keep accurate notes throughout.
3
Meet with the individual
Give them the opportunity to explain. Document the conversation. This is the fact find meeting — keep it focused and factual.
4
Consider the full context
Workload, supervision history, personal circumstances, training received, whether expectations were clearly communicated.
5
Decide and record next steps
Can it be resolved informally? If yes — agree actions, document, and review. If no — escalate to a formal investigation. Record your reasoning either way.

Informal resolution: Where a concern can be addressed without formal process — coaching, additional training, an agreed action plan — document it and set a review date. No further action needed if it resolves.

Never allow a fact find to become an informal disciplinary by the back door. Do not communicate a view about likely outcome to the individual during this stage.

A formal investigation is triggered when the fact find suggests there may be a case to answer that cannot be resolved informally. The investigator's role is to establish the facts — not to decide the outcome.

1
Appoint an impartial investigator
Someone with no prior involvement in the matter. This is not optional.
2
Set the terms of reference
Define clearly what is being investigated before it begins. Scope creep is one of the most common reasons investigations are challenged.
3
Notify the individual in writing
They must know the investigation is taking place, what it concerns, and who the investigator is.
4
Conduct witness interviews
Open, non-leading questions. Record accurately. Allow interviewees to review their account.
5
Compile the investigation report
Findings of fact only. No sanction recommendations — that is for the hearing manager who comes to the case fresh.

Key principle: The same process applies every time — regardless of who is involved. This consistency is what makes the process defensible and fair.

Disciplinary process
A structured four-step process for addressing conduct or performance concerns fairly and consistently.
✕
1
Send written invite clearly stating the allegations
The individual must know exactly what they are being asked to attend and what the allegations are before the hearing takes place.
2
Include a copy of the disciplinary policy and full evidence pack
They must have sight of all evidence that will be referred to in the hearing.
3
Give reasonable notice — minimum 48 hours
The individual must have adequate time to prepare their response and arrange a companion if they wish.
4
Inform of right to bring a companion
They have the right to be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative.
5
Confirm a note taker will be present
A note taker must be present throughout the hearing. This is separate from the chair.
1
Chair must be of appropriate seniority
The hearing must be chaired by someone with the authority to make the decision and with no prior involvement in the investigation.
2
Go through allegations and all evidence gathered
Present the allegations and evidence clearly and in full before asking the individual to respond.
3
Give the colleague full opportunity to respond
The individual must have genuine opportunity to respond to each allegation and present their account. Do not rush this stage.
4
Allow companion to make representations but not answer questions
The companion can support and speak on behalf of the individual but cannot answer questions directed at them.
5
Take detailed notes — share with colleague and confirm they are happy
Notes must be a full and accurate record of what was said. Share them with the individual after the hearing and ask them to confirm they are accurate.
1
Review all evidence carefully after the hearing
Do not make a decision during the hearing itself. Take time to consider everything properly.
2
Consider the colleague's response and any mitigating circumstances
What did they say in response to the allegations? Are there any factors that genuinely contextualise the conduct?
3
Consider any previous disciplinary history
Review any live warnings or previous matters that are relevant to the decision.
4
Consult HR before confirming outcome
Always consult HR before communicating the decision. Do not confirm an outcome to the individual before doing so.
5
Decision must be made within 7 working days of the hearing
Do not leave the individual waiting unnecessarily. Aim to communicate the decision as soon as possible within the 7 working day window.
1
Confirm decision and reasons in writing within one week
The outcome letter must be issued within one week of the hearing. It must clearly state the decision and the reasoning behind it.
2
State the nature of the complaint and required standards
Be clear about what the conduct concern was and what standard of behaviour or performance is expected going forward.
3
Include timescales for improvement where applicable
Where the outcome includes an expectation of improvement, set out clearly what is expected and by when.
4
Place record on personnel file
The outcome letter and hearing record must be placed on the individual's personnel file.
5
Inform of right to appeal within 5 working days
Every formal outcome must include the right of appeal. The individual has 5 working days to submit an appeal. The appeal must be heard by someone not involved in the original hearing.
Grievance
When a grievance is raised, your role is to make sure it is heard fairly, investigated properly, and responded to promptly. This is not about finding fault — it is about taking concerns seriously and resolving them as quickly as possible.
✕

When a grievance is submitted, acknowledge it promptly and arrange a meeting as soon as possible — usually within seven calendar days of receiving it.

1
Acknowledge receipt in writing
Confirm you have received the grievance and let the colleague know when they can expect to be invited to a meeting.
2
Appoint the right manager
The manager hearing the grievance must not be the subject of it and must have no prior involvement in the matter. If the grievance is about their line manager, escalate to a more senior manager.
3
Arrange the meeting promptly
Aim to hold the meeting within seven calendar days. If it needs to be rescheduled, rearrange within one week of the original date.
4
Confirm the right to be accompanied
The colleague can bring a work colleague or trade union representative to the meeting. Confirm this in the invite.

Grievances should be raised within one month of the issue taking place. If a grievance repeats a previous complaint where circumstances have not changed, you may decide not to hear it again.

1
Give the colleague full opportunity to set out their concern
Do not interrupt or jump to conclusions. Your role at this stage is to listen and understand the full picture before asking questions.
2
Ask clarifying questions
Make sure you fully understand what is being raised, what the colleague wants as an outcome, and what evidence or witnesses are relevant.
3
Take detailed notes throughout
Detailed notes must be taken during the meeting. Share them with the colleague afterwards and ask them to confirm they are accurate. The meeting must not be recorded on any device.
4
Carry out any further investigation needed
After the meeting, speak to any relevant witnesses, review any documentation, and gather the full picture before reaching an outcome.

After the meeting and any further investigation, write to the colleague with your outcome. There are three possible outcomes:

1
Grievance not upheld
You did not find sufficient evidence to support the grievance. Explain clearly why in the outcome letter.
2
Grievance fully upheld
You found evidence to support the grievance. Set out what action will be taken as a result.
3
Grievance partially upheld
You found evidence to support some but not all of the concerns raised. Be clear about which elements were upheld and which were not, and why.

Right of appeal: Every outcome letter must include the right of appeal. The colleague has seven calendar days to submit an appeal. The appeal must be heard by a manager not involved in the original grievance.

Collective grievances: Where a group of colleagues raise a grievance about the same issue, one grievance letter should be submitted on behalf of the group with all names and signatures. A spokesperson should be nominated to represent the group at the meeting.

Grievance raised during a disciplinary process: If the grievance is unrelated to the disciplinary matter, the disciplinary continues and a separate manager handles the grievance. If the two matters are connected, they may be dealt with together or the disciplinary may be paused.

Grievances from ex-colleagues: Still investigate fully, but a meeting will not always be required.

If a grievance is found to contain intentionally untrue allegations, or if a colleague is treated unfairly because they raised a legitimate concern, this may be dealt with under the disciplinary process.

Mitigating circumstances
Two people can make the same mistake and receive different outcomes. That is fairness, not inconsistency.
✕

Length of service and clean record; personal circumstances such as bereavement or illness; whether the person self-reported; whether they accepted responsibility early; gaps in training or unclear expectations; workload pressures beyond their control.

Same mistake — two different outcomes

Two care workers both fail to complete handover notes on the same shift.

Worker A has eight years of service, no previous warnings, was going through a bereavement, flagged it the next morning, and immediately accepted responsibility.

Worker B has three formal warnings in twelve months, was aware of the requirement, had refresher training six weeks prior, and offered no explanation.

The process is identical. The outcome is different. That is not inconsistency — it is the process working exactly as it should.

Claiming ignorance of a rule clearly communicated and signed off does not mitigate. An account inconsistent with witness evidence does not mitigate. Attributing blame to colleagues without evidence does not mitigate.

The test: does this factor genuinely explain why the conduct occurred — or is it an attempt to avoid accountability? Explanation is not the same as excuse.

1
Ask directly in the hearing
Specifically invite the individual to share anything they want you to take into account. Do not assume they will raise it themselves.
2
Consider it genuinely before deciding
Mitigating circumstances must be weighed before the decision — not noted afterwards as a formality.
3
Record your reasoning
Your outcome letter must explain what was raised, how you weighed it, and how it influenced your decision. This is what makes the outcome defensible.
Absence management
Supporting people back to work and managing attendance consistently — not penalising people for being unwell.
✕

Trigger point: 3 absences in a rolling six month period triggers an absence review meeting. This is not a disciplinary — it is a supportive conversation to understand what is happening and agree what support is needed.

1
Return to work conversation
On the first day back after every absence, however short. Supportive, not disciplinary. Welcome the person back, note the reason, consider whether any adjustments are needed. Always record it.
2
Absence review meeting — triggered at 3 in a rolling six months
Meet with the individual to explore the reasons behind the absences. Listen, ask open questions, and consider whether there is an underlying issue — health, personal circumstances, or something work-related. Document the meeting and agree any support or actions.
3
Review and monitor
Follow up on any agreed actions. If absence reduces, acknowledge it. If the pattern continues, make clear that further absence may trigger the next stage.
4
Escalation — a further 3 absences
Where absence continues to a further 3 in the rolling period despite support, the process escalates. This may move toward a formal stage. Written invitation, right to be accompanied, right of appeal.

Equality Act: Always assess whether absence may be linked to a disability before taking formal action. If it is, reasonable adjustments must be explored and documented first.

Manager guide
Return to work conversations
Every absence, however short, should be followed by a return to work conversation. Not as a deterrent — as genuine support.
✕
1
Welcome them back genuinely
Start by saying you are glad they are back. It sounds simple, but it matters — particularly for someone anxious about returning.
2
Ask how they are — properly
Not just "are you fit for work?" Some people return before they are truly ready. Create space for that conversation.
3
Note the reason for absence
Record what they tell you. You are not a doctor — record what was said and leave it at that unless a pattern is emerging.
4
Consider whether adjustments are needed
Particularly after a longer absence or one related to a health condition.
5
Brief them on anything they have missed
What has changed? Is there anything they need to know before they go back out?

The return to work conversation is not an interrogation and should never feel like one. Its purpose is support and connection — the monitoring function is secondary.

Managing performance
Most performance issues can be resolved without a formal process — if addressed early, honestly, and with genuine support.
✕
1
Name the concern early
The longer you leave it, the harder it becomes — and the more unfair it is to the person. If you have noticed something, say something.
2
Be clear about what good looks like
"You need to be better at communication" is not a standard. "Handover notes must be completed before the end of every shift" is.
3
Explore what is getting in the way
Is it a skill issue or a will issue? Someone who does not know how needs training. Someone who knows but chooses not to is a different conversation.
4
Set out a support plan
What are you going to do to help them improve? What does success look like and by when?
5
Review and follow up
Recognise improvement. If things are not improving despite genuine support, a formal process becomes appropriate — not before.

Document everything. Informal performance conversations must be recorded even if they do not go on the person's file. If the situation later escalates, you will need evidence that you tried informally first.

Probation
The probationary period is your opportunity to support a new colleague to settle in, meet the standard, and feel confident in their role. It must be managed actively — reviews must be completed, concerns raised early, and decisions made within the six month window.
✕
1
Day 1 — Employment begins
The colleague begins employment and completes induction and onboarding, including mandatory training, shadowing, and support from the recruitment officers.
2
Weeks 1—12 — Initial Be Supported Period
The colleague is actively supported throughout the first twelve weeks. The first probation review meeting is held at the three month mark and the PASS form is updated.
3
3 months — First probation review
Has performance been satisfactory? If yes — proceed to final review at six months. If no — hold a second probation review meeting at four to six months and update the PASS form.
4
6 months — Final probation review
Final probation review meeting held and PASS form updated. Confirm completion of the probationary period in writing.

Important: Reviews must be completed within six months of the start date. If this deadline passes without a review, the colleague passes probation by default.

Every probation review must be documented using the PASS form and updated on the system. Do not hold a verbal review without a written record.

1
Prepare before the meeting
Review performance, attendance, feedback from the care coach or team, and any concerns raised since the last review.
2
Be clear about what is expected
The colleague must know what the standard is and whether they are meeting it. Vague feedback is not fair to them and does not protect the organisation.
3
Update and share the PASS form
Complete the PASS form during or immediately after the meeting. Share it with the colleague and keep a copy on file.
4
Confirm the outcome in writing
Whether the colleague passes, moves to an extended period, or does not pass — confirm it in writing promptly.

If performance is unsatisfactory at the three month review, do not simply hope it improves. Hold the second probation review at four to six months and be honest about what needs to change.

1
Second probation review — 4 to 6 months
Only applies if the first review was unsatisfactory. Review performance again. If satisfactory — probation passed, confirm in writing. If not — move to the extension period.
2
Extended probation — 4 to 12 weeks
Only reached if performance was unsatisfactory at the second review. A further review is held at the end of the extension period.
3
Extended review outcome
Pass — confirm completion in writing. Fail — confirm termination in writing. Both outcomes must be confirmed promptly and handled with care.

Always consult HR before confirming a decision to terminate during or at the end of probation. Do not communicate this to the colleague before doing so.

Receiving a disclosure
When a member of staff tells you something serious, how you respond in that moment matters enormously.
✕
1
Listen without interrupting
Let them tell you what they need to. Do not ask lots of questions. Do not minimise. Do not share your own reactions in the moment.
2
Do not promise confidentiality
You cannot promise to keep this between you — and you must not. Tell them you are glad they told you and that you will need to take it further, but that you will handle it carefully.
3
Do not investigate yourself
Your role is to receive the disclosure and escalate it — not to start asking questions or forming conclusions.
4
Record what was said accurately
As soon as possible after — what they said, in their words as closely as you can recall, with the time and date.
5
Escalate promptly
To the registered manager, HR, or safeguarding lead depending on the nature of the disclosure. Do not sit on it overnight.

Look after yourself too. Receiving a serious disclosure is emotionally demanding. After you have done what needs to be done, use your own supervision to process it. You are not expected to absorb this alone.

New to management
The things nobody tells you when you step into your first management role in care.
✕

Your relationship with the team changes. You may have been friends with people you now manage. That dynamic shifts — not because you have changed, but because your responsibilities have. Being liked and being respected are different things. In management you need the second more than the first.

Consistency matters more than you think. Your team watches how you treat different people. If you are flexible with one person and firm with another in the same situation, trust will erode quietly long before you realise it is happening.

You do not have to have all the answers. "I don't know but I'll find out" is a perfectly good answer. What your team needs is honesty and follow-through — not omniscience.

Deal with things early. Almost every situation that becomes a significant HR matter started as a small thing someone hoped would resolve itself. It rarely does. The earlier you address a concern, the easier it is to resolve informally.

Use your own supervision. Management is isolating in a way that frontline care is not. The problems land with you and often cannot be shared widely. Use your supervision with your own manager to think through difficult situations — that is what it is for.

Remember why you were promoted. The skills that made you good at your previous role — your care for people, your attention to detail, your commitment to doing things right — are the same skills that will make you a good manager. Trust them.

Safeguarding
Everyone has a responsibility to safeguard the people we support. This belongs to every member of staff, every day.
✕
1
Recognise
Know the types of abuse — physical, emotional, financial, sexual, neglect, institutional, and modern slavery. Know the signs: unexplained injuries, withdrawal, changes in behaviour, financial irregularities.
2
Respond
If a service user discloses something, listen carefully. Do not promise confidentiality. Do not ask leading questions. Do not investigate — your role is to hear and report.
3
Record
Write down exactly what was said or observed as soon as possible, in the person's own words. Do not interpret or summarise.
4
Report
Report to your manager immediately. If there is immediate risk to life, call 999 first. Safeguarding concerns must never wait until the end of a shift.

Remember: You do not need proof to raise a concern. Something feeling wrong is enough. It is not your job to decide whether abuse has occurred — report what you have seen or heard and let the right people make that determination.

Record keeping
Good records protect the people we support, protect staff, and demonstrate the quality of our care.
✕

Records must be written at the time or as soon as possible after — factual, objective, free from opinion and jargon, and signed with your full name, date, and time.

The test: Could a colleague who has never met this person deliver safe, person-centred care based solely on what you have written? If not, it needs more detail.

"Raised her voice, paced the room, and declined her medication three times" is a record. "Appeared agitated" is an opinion.

Poor records are one of the most common findings in CQC inspections and the first thing reviewed in a safeguarding enquiry or legal challenge.

Equality & inclusion
Fair treatment is not a policy — it is how we work, every day, with every person.
✕

The Equality Act 2010 protects nine characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation.

Inclusive practice means actively checking whether our policies and day-to-day behaviours create barriers for any individual or group — and taking steps to remove them.

When making care decisions, consider whether a person's protected characteristics are being appropriately taken into account — religious dietary requirements, gender preferences for personal care, communication needs related to disability.

In people processes: Before taking any formal action, consider whether a protected characteristic could be a factor. If it could be, take advice before proceeding.

Communication
How we communicate shapes how people experience the service — and how much they trust us.
✕

With service users: use plain language, check understanding, do not rush, and be led by the person's preferred communication method.

With families: be honest, calm, and factual. Do not speculate or make promises you cannot keep. If a family member is distressed, listen first and escalate if needed.

With colleagues: be direct and respectful. If you have a concern about someone's practice, raise it — directly or with your manager. Concerns that circulate without being raised formally do not get resolved.

With professionals: be concise and factual. They need the person's name, date of birth, the concern, and what you need — not a lengthy narrative.

Mental Capacity Act
The MCA 2005 protects people's right to make their own decisions — and sets out how to act when they cannot.
✕
1
Presume capacity
Every adult has the right to make their own decisions unless it is established they lack capacity. Always start here.
2
Support the person to decide
Take all practicable steps to help someone decide for themselves before concluding they cannot.
3
Respect unwise decisions
A person has the right to make decisions others might consider unwise. An unusual choice does not mean someone lacks capacity.
4
Act in best interests
Any decision made on behalf of someone lacking capacity must be in their best interests — considering their past wishes, values, and the views of those close to them.
5
Least restrictive option
Always consider whether there is a less restrictive way to achieve the same outcome. Restriction must be proportionate and justified.

Deprivation of Liberty: If restrictions on a person's freedom are necessary for their safety and they lack capacity, a DoLS authorisation may be required. Speak to your manager if you believe this applies.

Whistleblowing
Speaking up is not disloyalty — it is one of the most important things any member of staff can do.
✕

Whistleblowing means raising a concern about wrongdoing or risk that affects others — unsafe care, fraud, illegal activity, harm to a service user, or any attempt to cover these up.

1
Raise it internally first
Speak to your manager. If the concern involves your manager, go to a more senior leader. Most concerns should be resolved within the organisation.
2
Use the whistleblowing policy
If you are not comfortable raising it verbally, raise it in writing. The policy sets out how it is handled and who responds.
3
Escalate externally if needed
If your concern has not been taken seriously internally, you can contact CQC, the local authority, or the NHS. You are protected by law for raising a genuine concern in good faith.

Your protection: The Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 protects workers who raise genuine concerns in good faith. You must not be dismissed, disciplined, or treated unfairly for raising a concern.

If a service user is at immediate risk: call 999 and report to your manager immediately. Do not wait for a formal process.
